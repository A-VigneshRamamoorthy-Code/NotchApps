Sweep your cursor toward the notch and a furry paw springs out to catch it — fluid, springy, genuinely animal-like motion. Wander off and it curls back to sleep.

A Verlet-rope limb springs from the notch point nearest your pointer and bats at it with real spring physics — never a canned, looping zoom.

The useful one · NotchLock

Pull the cord. Lock your Mac.

Slide your cursor to the notch and a pull-cord drops out on real physics. Grab the bead, pull it down until it glows red — armed — then let go. It snaps back, swings like a lamp chain, and your Mac locks.

Tug a real lamp-cord

A pinned Verlet rope drops, hangs and swings under gravity. Pull past the threshold and release → your screen locks instantly, the same call as macOS Lock Screen. Change your mind? Bring the bead back up before you let go.

Four cord styles

Brass bead, chunky thick rope, a metal ball-chain, or a glowing neon cord — switch anytime from the right-click menu. Your pick is remembered.

Survives reboot

Registers a self-healing LaunchAgent, so the cord is back after every restart or login — no setup, toggle it any time from the menu.

Zero permissions

Uses global event monitors — no Accessibility or Screen-Recording grants. The overlay is click-through, so it never steals a click.

~600 KB, drawn in code

Cord, bead, ring and app icon are all CoreGraphics — no asset files, no Electron. Pure SwiftPM, and it sleeps to 0% CPU when tucked away.

Good to know

Questions, answered

Are Notch Apps really free?
Yes — both NotchPaw and NotchLock are completely free and open-source. No account, no subscription, no ads, no tracking. If they made you smile, starring the repos (NotchPaw · NotchLock) genuinely helps.
Why does macOS block them on first open?
Both apps are open-source and ad-hoc signed (not yet notarized), so Gatekeeper may block the very first launch. Just right-click the app → Open → Confirm. You only need to do this once per app.
Can I run NotchPaw and NotchLock at the same time?
Absolutely. They're separate apps and coexist happily. NotchPaw plays around the notch, while NotchLock only drops its cord when your cursor comes close — so they stay out of each other's way.
Will they drain my battery or CPU?
No. Each uses 0% CPU when idle and only wakes while you're interacting with it — the animation loop sleeps the moment the paw or cord is tucked away.
What do I need to run them?
macOS 14 (Sonoma) or later. NotchPaw runs on Apple Silicon or Intel; NotchLock targets Apple Silicon. Both are happiest on MacBooks with a notch, and fall back to a top-center region on Macs without one.
How do I switch styles or quit?
Hover or right-click the notch. In NotchPaw, click to pick a different critter or quit. In NotchLock, right-click to change the cord style, toggle start-at-login, lock now, or quit. Your choices are remembered.
